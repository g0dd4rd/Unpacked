How to practice scales and intervals

First take your time and listen to whatever scale or arpeggio you're trying to learn. Have undisturbed, focused listening session, if possible. Second, try to sing the material first with the recording and then without it. The last step is transferring it to the instrument. You can try and really just follow your voice and find the notes on the instrument, or take bit of a shortcut and learn some common patterns. It's good to practice the pattern on one string in three or five noted chunks and then more strings the same way. In other words, work the pattern both vertically and horizontally. Once you have the pattern down and you can play it without any mistake for at least three minutes, try and improvise with it in the rhythms from first chapter of this book, or just completely freely on your own. After you feel comfortable, try to improvise the pattern over different chords - your favorite song for example, to have some harmonic context. This should give you enough freedom and prepare yourself for real jam.

Technique mechanics

When it comes to playing, we often use almost too much pressure. This can lead to RSI, bad intonation (tones are out of tune) and slowing us down in general. So how much pressure should we apply?

Just enough to get the tone out without any buzz. How do we achieve that, though?

  1. Just lightly touch the strings, outlining what you want to play wth your fretting hand and pick the muted strings with your right hand
  2. Repeat step 1, just pressing slightly more on the strings so they start buzzing while picking them
  3. Finally, repeat step 1, but this time press just enough to produce the correct tone and pitch

Repeat any of the steps or the whole exercise how many times you need to play with just the right amount of pressure.

Yes, modes

Modes? What are modes? Every scale is a mode. But let's rather think about modes as scales, as modes may freak out some of us. Every scale has a structure that gives the scale its character. The structure is denoted by distance between the tones or pitches, that is described as semitones, steps or intervals. The first scale we will look at is called a chromatic scale.

Chromatic scale

The chromatic scale contains all twelve semitones, denoting the whole octave from the root note to the same root note, but octave higher. That's it - just twelve tones, it's all we have in the western music. There's a pause or rest, often called the thirteenth tone and very important one. We can also create microtones with different techniques such as vibrato.

Simplified harmony

In the classical western music, the relationship between the first and fifth tone (also called a degree) in a scale is important. If we build chords from scale degrees, we get a key, creating a chord called tonic at the first degree and a dominant on the fifth degree. These two create the greatest harmonic tension, the farther we go from the tonic, greater the tension and greater the release if we go from the dominant straight back to the tonic. It could be said that the western music is myriads of strategies of how to move between these two harmonic functions. There's more than these two functions though, let's go through those too.

We'll explain everything now on an ionian mode in the key of C.

Harmonic function:         Tonic Supertonic Mediant Subdominant Dominant Submediant Leading
Scale degree:              I     ii         iii     IV          V        vi         vii
Chord quality:             Major minor      minor   Major       Dominant minor      half-diminished
Ionian mode chords:        C     Dmin       Emin    F           G        Amin       Bhdim
Ionian mode triads:        ceg   dfa        egb     fac         gbd      ace        bdf
Ionian mode 7th chords:    Cmaj7 Dmin7      Emin7   Fmaj7       Gdom7    Amin7      Bhdim7
Ionian mode 7th arpeggios: cegb  dfac       egbd    face        gbdf     aceg       bdfa

We've introduced the 7th chords just for the sake of outlining the harmonic functions better, especially in the case of the dominant, where it is not clear from the G chord because it essentially is a major chord and it gains its dominant harmonic function only in a given context. Let's treat the dominant chord as a special major chord for now, it will become clear to you after reading about the mixolydian mode.

Notice that there're only four types of chords in the ionian mode - major, minor, dominant and half-diminished. These are also probably the most used chords, definitely in the case of major, minor and dominant. Knowing this, we'll go over the modes that are related can be applied to these chords.

Aeolian mode - Natural minor scale

The aeolian mode, most commonly known as the natural minor scale, is the sixth mode derived from the respective degree of the ionian mode / natural major scale. This implies that if we play the aeolian mode / natural minor scale in two octaves, we can switch seamlessly to the ionian mode on the third degree, called a relative natural major scale. Example would be a C aeolian mode / natural minor scale, containing c,d, eb, f, g, ab, bb and Eb ionian mode / natural major scale - the relative major scale of the minor scale, containing the same notes, starting on eb, f, g, ab, bb, c, d. We can use this mode to play over chords like C minor (c-eb-g) or Eb major (eb-g-bb), creating more melodic, consonant lines and licks because it contains the same notes, or over a C major (c-e-g) or Eb minor (eb-gb-bb) chords, creating more bluesy and dissonant lines and licks because the minor third clashes with the major third. The minor / flat seventh in this mode can be used either to create dissonance against a major third and seventh in major chord (C ionian, c-e-g-b), or to express / outline a minor (C aeolian 7, c-eb-g-bb) or dominant chords (C mixolydian, c-e-g-bb), clashing with its minor third, but flattering its minor seventh). Mind you, this minor tonality characteristic / perception of the mode is common in western culture.

This scale has the following structure:

semitones (1 semitone is 1 fret on the fret board)
2-1-2-2-1-2-2

Let's take a specific scale, C aeolian mode - natural minor scale as an application:

Harmonizing scales - chord theory, circle of 4ths/5ths - all 12 keys

Scales are great, but how they relate to chords? Let's take a look at our friend - the natural major scale, or the ionian mode. If we wanted to know what chords can be created using this scale, comprising its key, we just have to take the first, third and fifth tone, also called a scale degree and repeat that for every and each. We will do just that and also take a look at the intervals involved.

natural major scale / ionian mode
1   3   5
c d e f g a b c
c e g -> 3 b3 -> C major

We took the first, third and fifth degree of the C natural major scale, or ionian mode, which gives us tones c, e and g. The distance between tones c and e is called an interval of majord third, the distance between e and g is called an interval of minor (flat) third. These three tones together give us the C major chord. Chord is a combination of three or more tones. Major chord is made of combination of a major third as its first interval and minor third as its second interval. The minor third is three semitones or frets and the major third is four semitones on the fret board. The chord derived from the first degree is called the tonic.

Next, we will derive rest of the chords in the scale.

  1   3   5
c d e f g a b c
d f a -> b3 3 -> D minor

This time, we started counting on d and we got tones d f and a, the d and f creating a minor third interval and f and a creating a major third interval. This specific combination of minor and major third intervals gives us a minor chord. Notice that this combination is the exact opposite of a major chord. The chord we obtained is D minor. Let's continue.


    1   3   5
c d e f g a b c
e g b -> b3 3 -> E minor


We've got again a minor chord, called E minor. So far we have a C major derived from the first degree, D minor derived from the second degree and E minor derived from the third degree of ionain mode, or natural major scale.


      1   3   5
c d e f g a b c
f a c -> 3 b3 -> F major



From the fourth degree, we've got again a major chord. Chord derived from the fourth degree is called a subdominant.


        1   3   5
c d e f g a b c d e f g a b c
g b d -> 3 b3 -> G major



On the fifth degree, we've got a major chord, called a dominant.


          1   3   5
c d e f g a b c d e f g a b c
a c e -> b3 3 -> A minor



On the sixth degree, we have a minor chord. If we start the scale or the key from this degree, we will get a relative minor scale or key of this major scale.


            1   3   5
c d e f g a b c d e f g a b c
b d f -> b3 b3 -> B half-diminished



This time, on the seventh degree, we've got sort of a special chord called half-diminished, comprising of two minor third intervals. This chord can sound dissonant and is often used as a passing chord (in-between chord), creating tension.


Here's everything in one table:


C natural major scale / ionian mode
c d e f g a b c
c e g -> 3 b3 -> C major
d f a -> b3 3 -> D minor
e g b -> b3 3 -> E minor
f a c -> 3 b3 -> F major
g b d -> 3 b3 -> G major
a c e -> b3 3 -> A minor
b d f -> b3 b3 -> B hdim



Notice how the chords move the notes vertically and diagonally (from left to top) , from the first, third and fifth degree. This movement is called voice leading, more obvious with seventh chords - chords that contain also the seventh degree of a scale.
